Sri Suktam Verse -1

Verse in Sanskrit:

हिरण्यवर्णां हरिणीं सुवर्णरजतस्रजाम्।
चन्द्रां हिरण्मयीं लक्ष्मीं जातवेदो म आवह।।

Sandhi Breakdown:

हिरण्यवर्णाम् हरिणीम् सुवर्णरजतस्रजाम्।
चन्द्राम् हिरण्मयीम् लक्ष्मीं जातवेदः म आवह।।

Source:

Śrī Sūktam, Rigvedic Hymn, traditionally part of the Khila Suktas of the Rigveda.

English Translation:

“Bring to me the golden-hued one, radiant and auspicious, adorned with garlands of gold and silver; the luminous, resplendent, and benevolent Goddess Lakshmi, O Jātaveda (Fire God).”

Transliteration:

Hiraṇyavarṇāṁ hariṇīṁ suvarṇarajatasrajām
Candrāṁ hiraṇmayīṁ lakṣmīṁ jātavedo ma āvaha.

Detailed Grammatical Analysis:

  1. Case Analysis:

    • Many words are in the accusative case, indicating they are the objects of the verb आवह (āvaha) (bring forth). Examples: हिरण्यवर्णाम्, हरिणीम्, लक्ष्मीम्.
    • जातवेदः is in the vocative case, as the speaker addresses the Fire God directly.
    • म (ma) is in the accusative case, as the request is “to me.”

  2. Verb Analysis:

    • The verb आवह (āvaha) is in the imperative mood, second person singular, expressing a command or request.

  3. Compound Analysis:

    • हिरण्यवर्णाम्: Karmadhāraya compound meaning “golden-hued.”
    • सुवर्णरजतस्रजाम्: Bahuvrīhi compound describing “one whose garlands are of gold and silver.”
    • जातवेदः: Tatpuruṣa compound meaning “knower of all beings.”

  4. Sandhi Observations:

    • जातवेदः म → जातवेदो म: The visarga ः changes to ओ before म, following sandhi rules for smoother recitation.

  5. Vedic vs. Classical Sanskrit Usage of “म (Ma)”:

    The word म (ma) in the phrase जातवेदो म आवह is consistent with Vedic Sanskrit and is not exclusive to Classical Sanskrit.

    In Vedic Sanskrit, म (ma) is frequently used as the accusative form of the pronoun अहम् (aham), meaning “me” or “to me.”

    The Vedic hymns often address deities with म (ma) to express personal devotion or to seek blessings, making its use in this verse entirely appropriate and not derived from Classical Sanskrit.

The word हरिणीम् can mean “deer-like” or “radiant”, depending on the context. Let us analyze its usage in the verse हिरण्यवर्णां हरिणीं to determine the appropriate meaning.

Derivation of हरिणीम्:

  1. Root word: हरिणी

    • Derived from हरिण (hariṇa), which primarily means “deer.”
    • The feminine form हरिणी (hariṇī) can mean “a female deer” or something deer-like.
    • हरि (Hari) also implies “shining” or “radiant,” giving हरिणी an alternate meaning of “radiant one.”

  2. Grammatical Form:

    • हरिणीम् is in the accusative singular feminine form, as it is the direct object of the verb आवह (āvaha) in the verse.

Contextual Meaning:

In Śrī Sūktam, हरिणीम् does not refer to a literal deer. Instead, it symbolizes qualities associated with deer—gentle, graceful, delicate, and auspicious.

  • The term हरिणीम् in this verse aligns with radiance, grace, and auspiciousness, describing Goddess Lakshmi.
  • The idea of “radiant” connects with Lakshmi’s luminous and divine nature, consistent with other epithets in the verse like हिरण्यवर्णां (golden-hued) and हिरण्मयीं (resplendent).

Cultural Interpretation:

In Sanskrit poetry and scriptures, deer-like qualities often metaphorically describe gentleness, elegance, and auspiciousness, especially when referring to deities or divine beings. The term हरिणीम् here emphasizes both the radiance and grace of Goddess Lakshmi.

Conclusion:

In this verse, हरिणीम् should be understood as “radiant, graceful, and auspicious,” capturing the divine beauty and elegance of Goddess Lakshmi. While “deer-like” is a literal meaning, the intended sense here is metaphorical and symbolic of graceful radiance.

Phonetic Features:

  • Vowel Lengths: Long vowels like आं add a sense of auspiciousness and rhythm.
  • Sandhi Transitions: Smooth connections between words like जातवेदः म → जातवेदो म ensure melodic chanting.

Cultural Significance:

  1. Lakshmi’s Symbolism:

    • Goddess Lakshmi represents wealth, prosperity, and auspiciousness.
    • Her golden hue and radiant garlands symbolize abundance and divine beauty.

  2. Invocation of Agni (Fire God):

    • Agni (Jātaveda) is invoked as the intermediary who carries offerings to the deities, bridging the human and divine realms.
    • This reflects Vedic rituals where fire is central to yajñas (sacrificial offerings).

  3. Garlands of Gold and Silver:

    • The gold and silver garlands symbolize material and spiritual wealth, showing the dual nature of prosperity in the material and divine realms.

Philosophical Significance:

  1. Connection of Divine and Devotee:

    • The verse emphasizes humility, as the devotee requests the divine through Jātaveda’s assistance.
    • "Bring to me" reflects a longing for inner abundance, not just material wealth.

  2. Wealth as a Tool for Dharma:

    • Lakshmi’s blessings are sought not for greed but for fulfilling dharma (righteous duties), showing the spiritual perspective on material prosperity.

  3. Unity of Material and Spiritual:

    • The description of Lakshmi blends material beauty (gold, silver) with spiritual radiance (resplendent and luminous), symbolizing that true wealth is holistic.
